If you are doing something no one has done before, no one likely cared enough to do it, unless you believe you are really just that competent. And if you are not doing anything new, then that task is not worth your time, as someone else has already done it better than you.

The trouble with all of this is that creating something sufficiently new is always going to be a club of one, and you have no way of knowing who the few people working on the same problem is, or if they even exist at all. If they do, they are likely so invested in their own approach and projects that they do not wish to do any more than talk to you (if you are lucky).

## Whether you need another person doesn't matter

**Because you can't find them.** If you thought the Pareto principle was brutal (where 20% of people produce 80% of results), welcome to the internet, where the ratio is even more skewed, and simply known as the [1% rule](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/276452171_Describing_the_distribution_of_engagement_in_an_Internet_support_group_by_post_frequency_A_comparison_of_the_90-9-1_Principle_and_Zipf%27s_Law/fulltext/54379c510cf2027cbb20256a/Describing-the-distribution-of-engagement-in-an-Internet-support-group-by-post-frequency-A-comparison-of-the-90-9-1-Principle-and-Zipfs-Law.pdf). The numbers game is brutal:

1. Non-advertising click-through rates are generally 2-10% for video [Google](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/7628154?hl=en#zippy=%2Cwhat-should-i-avoid-doing-with-my-click-through-rate-data%2Cwhy-does-my-video-have-a-high-click-through-rate-and-average-view-duration-but-low-impressions%2Chow-do-impressions-relate-to-monetization%2Cwhy-doesnt-the-click-through-rate-metric-match-my-calculations%2Cwhy-do-i-have-more-views-than-impressions%2Chow-do-i-know-if-my-impressions-click-through-rate-is-high-or-low) and 11-13% for chat apps/SMS [Gallup](https://news.gallup.com/opinion/methodology/221159/using-text-messaging-reach-survey-respondents.aspx). So if you're typing on Discord, don't expect anymore than maybe 1 out of 10 people to respond to you (even if you are friended with them).
2. The percentage of 'instaleavers' make up [~40%](https://aisel.aisnet.org/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1556&context=amcis2001) of community members. This may or may not be applicable depending on whether it is your own community.
3. ~1% of those who stay are contributors to a community.
4. ~10% of the remainder will be technically qualified and interested enough to serve as contributors to your specific projects.

In the most generous case, you are looking at between 0,0052% and 0,0008% of people you come into contact with being interested and capable enough to contribute to your projects, in other words, you would have to meet 125 thousand people before having a chance of finding anyone else to work with. 

Of course the ideal would be that work between contributors would be pooled such that it is a mutual exchange, and not just you leeching off of their contributions, but these people don't exist - not even in Plato's world of perfect forms.

## The One Man Tech Stack

Given that the internet is essentially dead to you, and horizontal integration is out of the question, all remaining efficiency must be gained via leveraging vertical integration. This is sort of only possible if you control your stack, i.e. all the technologies you use from beginning to end.

The existing problem is that developers have worked long and hard on horizontal integration, i.e. creating thousands upon thousands of discrete libraries and modules within said libraries, as opposed to vertical integration (which game engines are an excellent example of).

There are two main ways in which you can control your stack - one is somehow achieving power user zen with all the libraries or bits of software in your stack and setting up a fragile, but leveraged workflow that is able to accomplish more with off-the-shelf components, and the other is the permanent approach, i.e. building your own software engine and superapps that create things for you.

In any long-term situation, the former becomes incredibly difficult to maintain as fragmented ecosystems constantly shift under your feet, whilst the latter comes with immense responsibility; but if set up well, also immense power.
